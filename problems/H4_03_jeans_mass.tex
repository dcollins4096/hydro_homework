Jeans Mass.
\begin{enumerate}
	\item \pts{3} What is the Jeans Mass for a cloud of molecular hydrogen gas at 10 K and $1000
     \rm{cm}^{-3}$ (that's particles of molecular hydrogen per cc.)
     \item \pts{3} What is the Jeans Mass for $100 \rm{cm}^{-3}$ and $10^7$K?
     (In the early universe, the gas can't cool, so this is more appropriate
     for the first generation of stars)

\solution{
     \emph{Solution}

     Using (7.60) from the book, 

     \begin{align}
         c_s &= \sqrt{\frac{k_b T}{m_{x}}} = 1.9\sci{5}\cms\nn\\
         m_x &= \rm{mass\ of\ partcle} \nn\\
         &= 2.3 m_p = 3.84\sci{-24}\rm{g}\nn\\
         \rho_0 &= 1000 m_x = 3.84\sci{-21}  \rm{g}\ \percc\nn\\
         L_j &= 2 \pi \sqrt{c_s^2/(4 \pi G \rho_0)} = 2.09\sci{19} \rm{cm} = 0.68 pc \nn\\
         M_j &= \frac{4 \pi}{3} L_j^3 \rho = 1.48\sci{35}\rm{g} = 74 M_\odot\nn
     \end{align}
     Realistically one should use $L_j/2$ in the definition of $M_j$, which
     brings the answer down by a factor of 8.

     The scaling provided by the great font of knowledge, Wikipedia, gives
     \begin{align}
         M_j = 2 M_\odot 
         \left(\frac{c_s}{0.2
         \kms}\right)\left(\frac{n}{1000\percc}\right)^{-1/2}.
     \end{align}
     This is obtained by using
     \begin{align}
         %c_s &= 0.2 \kms\nn\\
         \rho&=3.9\sci{-21}\rm{g}\percc\nn\\
         L_j &= \frac{c_s}{\sqrt{G \rho}}\nn\\
         M_j &= \frac{4 \pi}{3}\rho (L_j/2)^3\nn
     \end{align}
     which gives 1.9 $M_\odot$ for the jeans mass of the cloud.  (Slightly
     smaller due to the slightly smaller sound speed)


     Using (7.60) I find $M_j = 5.14\sci{44}\rm{g}= 2.6\sci{11}M_\odot$.

     Using the version with fewer factors of $\pi$, I get 
     $M_j=1.15\sci{43}\rm{g}=5.8\sci{9}M_\odot$
}
     \end{enumerate}
